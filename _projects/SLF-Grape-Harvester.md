# SLF Mechanical Repellant from Grape Harvester

**Team:** Lanternfly Killers  
**Client(s):** Cornell CALS Extension / E\&J Gallo Winery / National Grape

---

## Problem statement (most important)

Spotted lanternflies (SLF) pose a serious contamination risk during grape harvest. Just 1–2 adult SLF can taint one kilogram of grape slurry, making the product unusable. Because SLF populations are highly prevalent in vineyards, contamination can rapidly scale, leading to substantial crop losses.

Farmers are actively seeking methods to manage SLF populations without reducing crop yield or introducing chemical contaminants. A key point of contamination occurs during mechanical harvesting, when SLF present on vines are pulled into the harvester along with grapes.

The challenge is that current solutions rely heavily on chemical spraying, which may contaminate the grape product, require repeated application, and add recurring costs. We aim to focus specifically on preventing SLF from entering the harvester during harvest without damaging grapes or vines.

---

## Impact

Reducing SLF contamination at the point of harvest directly supports farmers financially by preventing unnecessary crop loss. It also reduces production disruptions in processing facilities and increases overall grape product availability.

Benefits include:

- Reduced wasted crop yield
- Lower operational disruptions at processing facilities
- Increased product availability in the market
- Reduced reliance on chemical deterrents
- Potential long-term cost savings for vineyards

This solution supports growers, processing facilities, distributors, and consumers.

---

## Proposed direction(s)

### Concept A (Primary): Forward-Mounted Mechanical Blower

**What it is:**  
A blower system mounted to the front of the grape harvester that directs controlled airflow toward vines to mechanically dislodge SLF before grapes are harvested.

**How it would be used:**
- Mounted at the front of the harvester facing outward toward vines.
- A fan system produces steady, controlled airflow directed at grape clusters.
- SLF detach from vines due to airflow irritation and are pushed away from the intake path.
- Airflow prevents displaced SLF from re-entering the harvester.

**Why it's better than the status quo:**
- Avoids chemical contamination of grapes.
- Reduces recurring pesticide costs.
- Physically removes SLF instead of merely deterring them.
- Can be integrated into existing harvesting workflow.

**End-of-semester proof-of-concept:**
- Bench-top prototype blower assembly
- Controlled airflow testing on grape clusters
- Measurement of required airflow to detach SLF without dislodging grapes
- Computational estimation of required force based on SLF grip strength

---

### Concept B: Vibration Attraction Towers

**What it is:**  
Freestanding vibrating towers placed around vineyard perimeters that attract SLF using 60 Hz vibration and direct them into electrified traps.

**How it would be used:**
- Towers positioned strategically around vineyard perimeter.
- Mechanical vibration devices operate at 60 Hz to attract SLF.
- Electrified outer cage eliminates SLF upon contact.
- Collection container captures SLF for removal.

**Why it's better than the status quo:**
- Provides year-round population control.
- Reduces need for continuous chemical spraying.
- Requires no modification to harvesting equipment.
- Potential one-time infrastructure investment.

**End-of-semester proof-of-concept:**
- Small-scale vibrating structure prototype
- Controlled attraction testing
- Evaluation of field placement feasibility

---

## Key risks / unknowns

- **Damage to grapes or vines**  
  *Why it matters:* If airflow dislodges grapes, yield loss outweighs benefits.  
  *How we'll test it:* Controlled wind exposure testing on grape clusters to determine detachment thresholds.

- **Lanternflies becoming lodged in blower system**  
  *Why it matters:* Could clog or damage equipment.  
  *How we'll test it:* Evaluate multiple tube geometries and airflow configurations.

- **Insufficient blower force to detach SLF**  
  *Why it matters:* Ineffective removal makes system nonviable.  
  *How we'll test it:* Measure SLF weight and estimated grip force to calculate required airflow.

- **Field feasibility of vibration towers**  
  *Why it matters:* Vineyard layout may limit implementation.  
  *How we'll test it:* Consult client on spacing, mobility, and installation constraints.

---

## Questions for the client

1. Do SLF typically remain attached to vines during mechanical harvest?  
   *Decision affected:* Determines required airflow intensity and grape protection constraints.

2. When SLF contamination occurs in processing facilities, are operations halted for cleaning?  
   *Decision affected:* Clarifies economic and operational impact of contamination.

3. Is there available space within vineyard layouts to install permanent or semi-permanent structures?  
   *Decision affected:* Determines feasibility of vibration tower solution.

4. During peak infestation periods, what is the typical SLF density per row?  
   *Decision affected:* Influences airflow requirements and system scale.
